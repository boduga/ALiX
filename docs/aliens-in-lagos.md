# The Third Mainland Visitor

## A Story of Aliens in Lagos

---

### 1.

The first sign that something was wrong came at 5:47 AM on a Tuesday, when the Third Mainland Bridge decided to float.

Not *collapse* — that would have been mundane, almost expected. No, the bridge simply... rose. Two kilometers of concrete and steel, carrying the morning rush of danfos, okadas, and gleaming SUVs, lifted three meters above the lagoon surface and stayed there, suspended by nothing anyone could see.

The traffic jam that followed was biblical in proportion.

---

### 2.

Chidi Okafor had been stuck on the bridge for twenty-seven minutes when his phone buzzed. He ignored it — he was a software engineer at a fintech startup in Victoria Island, and the CTO had been breathing down everyone's necks about the payment gateway outage. But the bridge was floating, and frankly, the payment gateway could wait.

"Oga, wetin dey happen?" asked the danfo driver beside him, leaning out his window. The man's name, according to the motto painted on his bus, was *God'sTime Is The Best*. Twenty passengers crammed inside, some looking annoyed, some looking terrified, one woman calmly braiding a child's hair.

Chidi shook his head. "I no know."

His phone buzzed again. Then again. Then the collective sound of a thousand phone notifications rising from the bridge like a swarm of electronic bees.

A Facebook post from his cousin in Surulere: *"Una see the bridge???"*

A WhatsApp voice note from his mother in Enugu: *"Chidi, my son, are you alive? I saw it on Channels TV—the bridge is flying!"*

A tweet from some influencer: *"LAGOS IS CURSED 💀💀💀 #ThirdMainlandLevitation"*

And then, cutting through the noise, an SMS from the National Emergency Management Agency:

> **NEMA ALERT: AERIAL OBJECT DETECTED OVER LAGOS LAGOON. CIVILIANS ADVISED TO REMAIN INDOORS. DO NOT APPROACH WATERFRONT AREAS.**

Chidi looked up. The morning sky over Lagos was its usual hazy gray, a cocktail of harmattan dust and vehicular exhaust. But something was descending through the clouds, something large and metallic and silent.

Not silent, he realized. It was humming. A low, resonant frequency he could feel in his teeth.

"Omo," the danfo driver whispered, crossing himself, "na alien be dis o."

---

### 3.

By noon, the thing had landed.

It stood in the middle of the lagoon, half-submerged, roughly the size of the National Stadium. It looked like a giant geometric seed — all sharp angles and dark, matte surfaces that seemed to drink light rather than reflect it. No windows. No doors. No visible means of propulsion.

The military cordoned off the entire waterfront from Victoria Island to Ikoyi. Helicopters circled at a cautious distance. CNN called it "The Lagos Anomaly." The Nigerian Air Force tried bombing it; the bombs simply... stopped. They didn't explode. They didn't bounce off. They just halted in mid-air, hung there for a moment, and then gently fell into the lagoon.

The military tried again with missiles. Same result.

The internet, predictably, lost its collective mind.

*"Them don land for Ikeja, I swear."*

*"Alien don land for Lagos but e no fit cashout from GTBank because of Naira scarcity."*

*"If this alien try carry me go planet Mars, I beg carry me go Canada first."*

---

### 4.

They called her Professor Amina Bello-Lawal because she was the only person in the room who could authentically claim all three titles. Professor of Astrophysics at the University of Lagos. Director of the African Space Research Institute. And, as she was fond of reminding people, the woman who had predicted this exact scenario in a 2019 paper that the government had dismissed as "too speculative."

Now she stood in a military command center at the Naval Dockyard, surrounded by generals, politicians, and a very nervous-looking American CIA liaison who kept suggesting they "nuke it from orbit."

"With what?" Amina had asked him. "We don't have nukes. And even if we did, would it work? Did your bombs work? I didn't think so. Sit down and let me talk to them."

"Them?" the general asked. "You can talk to them?"

Amina adjusted her glasses — thick, black-framed, held together with tape on one side. "I can try."

---

### 5.

She approached the object in a military boat, accompanied by a young naval officer who was trying very hard not to look terrified.

"Ma, wetin I go do if dem attack?" he asked.

"Pray," Amina said. "Or swim. Whichever is faster."

When they were fifty meters away, the surface of the object rippled. A section of it simply dissolved, revealing an opening. Not a doorway — an *absence*, a hole in reality that led into darkness.

"Ma—"

"Stay here. If I'm not back in an hour, tell my daughter I left the remote for the DStv on the kitchen counter."

She stepped inside.

---

### 6.

The interior was not dark. It was filled with a soft golden light that seemed to come from everywhere and nowhere at once. The air was cool and smelled faintly of ozone and — impossibly — freshly fried plantains.

Amina walked forward, her sandals making no sound on the floor. The corridor curved and shifted, geometry behaving in ways that made her eyes hurt. She had studied non-Euclidean spaces in theory; experiencing one was something else entirely.

A voice spoke. Not in words she could hear, but in meanings that simply appeared in her mind.

> **YOU ARE THE ONE WHO LISTENS.**

Amina stopped. "I am a scientist. I listen to the universe. It seemed relevant."

> **YOUR SPECIES CALLS US ALIENS. WE ARE NOT. WE ARE YOUR DESCENDANTS.**

She blinked. "Come again?"

> **WE ARE HUMAN. FROM YOUR FUTURE. FIVE THOUSAND YEARS FROM NOW.**

Amina sat down on the floor — partly from shock, partly because her seventy-year-old knees were complaining. "Okay. Start from the beginning."

> **HUMANITY EVOLVED. HUMANITY SPREAD TO THE STARS. HUMANITY FORGOT ITS ORIGIN. WE WANTED TO REMEMBER. WE BUILT A MACHINE TO TRAVEL BACKWARD IN TIME, TO THE BEGINNING. TO HERE. TO LAGOS.**

"Why Lagos?"

> **BECAUSE THIS IS WHERE IT ALL BEGAN. NOT THE FIRST HUMANS. BUT THE FIRST HUMANS WHO MATTERED. IN YOUR TIME, THIS PLACE IS CHAOS. IN OUR TIME, IT IS LEGEND. THE CITY THAT NEVER STOPPED. THE PEOPLE WHO BUILT THE FUTURE OUT OF SCRAPS AND SONG.**

Amina felt tears prick her eyes. She wasn't sure why.

"There are five thousand years between us and you," she said. "What happens? Between now and then?"

> **MANY THINGS. SOME BEAUTIFUL. SOME TERRIBLE. BUT THE BEAUTIFUL ONES ARE WORTH IT.**

"And the bridge? Why did you lift the bridge?"

> **WE NEEDED YOUR ATTENTION. WE TRIED SENDING MESSAGES THROUGH YOUR NETWORKS. YOU IGNORED THEM. YOU ARE VERY BUSY. WE TRIED MAKING PATTERNS IN YOUR AGRICULTURAL FIELDS. A FARMER IN OYO HIT US WITH A BROOM. WE DECIDED A BRIDGE WOULD BE HARDER TO IGNORE.**

Despite everything, Amina laughed. It was a full, rich laugh that echoed through the impossible corridor.

"You're not wrong," she said.

---

### 7.

The object left three days later. It simply rose from the lagoon, silent and smooth, and ascended into the clouds until it was gone.

The Third Mainland Bridge settled back into its foundations with a gentle thud. Engineers inspected it and declared it structurally sound — somehow *more* sound than before, as if the levitation had realigned its inner stresses.

Professor Amina Bello-Lawal wrote a paper. No one believed her, of course. But she didn't care. She had received a gift — knowledge that the chaos of Lagos, the beautiful, maddening, relentless energy of the city, was not a bug but a feature. It was the seed of something great.

She sat on her balcony in Yaba, a cup of hot tea in her hand, watching the sun set over the lagoon. The danfos honked. The generators hummed. Someone was playing Burna Boy at full volume three blocks away. A goat was eating her gardenia bush.

Lagos was, as always, Lagos.

And somewhere out there, five thousand years in the future, her descendants were watching back.

She smiled, sipped her tea, and let the beautiful chaos wash over her.

---

**THE END**
